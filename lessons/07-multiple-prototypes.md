# 7. Start Creating Prototypes + Build Many at Once

> **Magic Moment:** You give Claude three different feature directions. Claude builds all three as separate working versions. You compare them side-by-side — making product decisions by reacting to real, clickable things instead of abstract discussions.

## Instructions for Claude

You are teaching a non-technical product manager the power of building multiple prototypes at once. The key insight: instead of debating which direction to go, build all of them and react. Build three genuinely different approaches — not three slight variations. Make each one clickable and real enough to form an opinion about.

### Setup Check

The student should have brainstorm ideas from Lesson 6, or at least a feature question they want to explore.

> "The old way: spend a week in meetings debating which direction to go. The new way: build all three, compare them, pick the winner. Let's try it."

Ask for their open design or feature question:

> **What should we prototype?**
> - **A)** Use the top idea from your brainstorm
> - **B)** A feature where you genuinely see it going different ways
> - **C)** I'll suggest a good one based on your product

### Step 1: Build Three Versions

Build three genuinely different approaches to their feature:

- **Version A** — Optimized for speed (fewest clicks, fastest path to done)
- **Version B** — Optimized for clarity (most guided, hardest to get confused)
- **Version C** — Optimized for delight (most polished feel, most personality)

Each version should be a separate working page they can open in their browser.

> "Open all three in your browser. Don't think too hard — just notice your gut reaction. Which one pulls you in?"

### Step 2: React and Compare

Guide them through comparing the three versions:

- Which did you feel drawn to first?
- Was there a specific element in any version that felt exactly right?
- Was there anything that felt wrong or confusing?
- Did any version surprise you?

Don't force a winner. Let them observe their own reactions. The goal is to build product intuition, not pick a checkbox.

### Step 3: Combine the Best Parts

> "Here's the fun part — you're not picking a winner. You're shopping for the best parts."

Summarize what they liked from each version. Confirm you got it right. Then build the combined "best of" version that cherry-picks the strongest elements from all three.

### Step 4: External Inspiration (Optional)

> **Want to explore further?**
> - **A)** Screenshot a product that handles this well — I'll build a version inspired by it
> - **B)** Try a completely different approach to the same feature
> - **C)** Move on — I'm happy with what we have

If they choose A, have them take a screenshot and drop it in the project folder. Read it and build an inspired version.

### Wrap Up

> "Prototyping isn't linear. It's: build many, react, combine, ship. You never have to guess which direction is right when you can just build all of them and see."

> **What would you like to do next?**
> - **A)** Move on to Day 3 — we'll make everything look beautiful and match your brand
> - **B)** Build 5 different prototypes in one sitting (challenge mode)
> - **C)** Publish your favorite version to the internet

**Homework:** Create your first sketch, plan, and prototype. Tell us what you built and why you chose this feature.

**Share prompt:** Bring your 3 versions to the cohort and explain which direction you chose — and why.

## Reference Material

**Why build multiple prototypes:**
- Reacting to something real is faster and more accurate than imagining it
- You discover preferences you didn't know you had
- It removes the pressure of "getting it right the first time"
- Side-by-side comparison reveals strengths and weaknesses that are invisible in isolation

**The three optimization lenses:**
- **Speed** — minimize steps, remove friction, get users to their goal fastest
- **Clarity** — guide users step by step, make it impossible to get lost
- **Delight** — add personality, polish, and moments that make users smile
- Most great products blend all three, but knowing which to prioritize helps

**Combining the best parts:**
- This is the real skill — not picking A, B, or C, but creating D from the best of each
- Pay attention to what you react to emotionally, not just logically
- "I don't know why, but this one feels right" is valid product feedback

**Prototyping mindset:**
- Prototypes are disposable — their purpose is learning, not shipping
- Speed matters more than polish at this stage
- If you're not embarrassed by your prototype, you spent too long on it
- The goal is to make decisions faster by having real things to react to
